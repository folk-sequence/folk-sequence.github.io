The major record labels are currently suing Suno and Udio, the two largest companies that generate music from text prompts, for somewhere in the neighborhood of three billion dollars. The suit alleges that the companies trained their generation models on copyrighted recordings without permission, and the suit is almost certainly correct on that narrow factual point. The artists are running a campaign called Say No To Suno. The radio company iHeartRadio has launched a program called Guaranteed Human, which promises that the songs they play are made by people. The fight, in other words, is on, and the fight is shaping up around the question of whether the AI music companies stole training data and whether AI-generated music should be allowed to compete with human music for streaming royalties.

The fight is being conducted in legal and economic terms, which are the terms that the labels and the streaming companies and the artist coalitions know how to fight in. I think the legal and economic terms are mostly correct. The training data was probably stolen. The royalty pool is being diluted. The companies should probably lose the lawsuit, and if they do not lose the lawsuit they should lose the public argument. None of that is what I want to write about. What I want to write about is the assumption underneath the entire fight, which is that AI-generated music is music, just made faster and cheaper than the old way, and that the dispute is therefore about market share and credit and compensation. That assumption is what almost everyone in the fight is taking for granted, and it is wrong, and the wrongness of it is hard to see because the output of the AI music companies sounds, to most listeners on most playback systems, like music.

Here is the thing that is not music. The thing that is not music is the production of an audio file by a system that made no decisions in the human sense of the word. The system observed what other audio files looked like statistically and produced an audio file that fits the same statistical distribution. There were no choices. There was no taste. There was no moment in which the person making the file weighed two options and picked one because it felt right. There was, in fact, no person making the file, only a person typing a description and pressing a button. The audio file is not the product of music-making. It is the product of music-shaped pattern matching, and the pattern matching is to actual music as a wax fruit is to actual fruit. They look similar from a distance and they perform a similar role in a still-life arrangement and they are different things, made of different stuff, with different properties.

The standard objection to this argument is that the brain doing the listening cannot tell the difference, so the difference does not matter. The objection is correct about brains and incorrect about why the difference matters. The difference matters not because the listener can tell while listening but because of what music is for, downstream of the listening. Music is for, among other things, connecting people across time. The listener experiences a song as the artifact of another person's specific situation, the choices that person made, the things they were paying attention to, the emotional state they were in when they made the recording. This experience is not always conscious, but it is what makes a song feel like more than a sequence of pleasant frequencies. The song is the trace of a human mind doing something. AI-generated audio has no human mind in its history. The trace is missing. The thing the song was for, in the deeper sense, is not there.

The opposite of AI-generated music is not human music, which is the wrong contrast. The opposite of AI-generated music is decision-making music. The contrast that matters is not who or what produced the audio file but whether decisions were made in the production. A drum machine left running on a default pattern is not decision-making music. A field recording uploaded without selection is not decision-making music. Most of what comes out of a major-label songwriter camp where the song is assembled to match a target playlist is barely decision-making music either. AI-generated music is the limit case of the no-decision pattern, but the pattern existed before AI got involved, and the fight against AI music is going to fail to address the pattern unless the people fighting it are willing to be honest about how much non-AI music had already moved in the same direction.

There is a similar but different concept that often gets confused with this one, which is the idea that AI music lacks soul. The soul framing is religious-adjacent and easy to wave away as sentimental, and people who use it tend to lose arguments with people who care about the empirical question of whether you can hear the difference. The argument I am making is not the soul argument. The soul argument is about a property the music possesses. My argument is about a property the production process possesses. The process either contained decisions or it did not. The presence of decisions in the process is not detectable in the audio file directly. It is detectable in the relationship between the file and the listener over time, in whether the file rewards repeated listening, in whether the file means something different ten years from now than it means today, in whether the file connects to a body of work that has its own internal logic. AI-generated audio does not reward repeated listening in this way and does not develop new meanings over time and does not connect to a body of work, because there is no body and there is no work, only outputs sampled from a distribution. The distribution does not have a history.

The fight as currently constructed will probably end in a settlement where the AI music companies pay licensing fees to the labels, which means the labels will profit from AI music while the artists who originally made the training data will receive almost nothing, and the AI companies will continue producing audio files that sound like music and that fill streaming playlists at zero marginal cost. The legal fight will be over and the underlying problem will not be addressed, because the legal fight is about who gets paid for the output and the underlying problem is that the output is not music.

The thing I am doing on this channel is the maximum opposite of AI music. Every video is a continuous recording of decisions being made in real time. You can watch the second-guessing. You can watch the moments where I change my mind. You can watch the dead air where I am thinking. You cannot fake any of that with a generation model, because the thing the generation model is missing is exactly the thing the recording is showing. The recording is the proof of decision-making. That is the only thing it is for. If you want music that is made of decisions, you can watch this channel, and if you do not, you can listen to whatever the playlist generators are serving you, and the difference between the two will not be detectable in any single moment, and it will be the entire point.
