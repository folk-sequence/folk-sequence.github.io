Every song has a stretch in the middle of it where the energy threatens to fall apart. The opening of the song establishes the idea, the ending of the song resolves the idea, and the middle is the part that has to keep the listener interested while neither establishing nor resolving anything. The middle is the hardest part of a song to write. The middle is also the part that producers most often handle badly, and the way they handle it badly is so consistent and so reflexive that I want to spend an essay describing the pattern, because once you can see the pattern you stop falling into it, and the songs you make get better in a way that has nothing to do with talent or tools.

Here is the pattern. The producer is in the middle of a song, the energy starts to feel uncertain, and the producer reaches for an addition. They drop in another instrument, or they layer a counter-melody, or they introduce a percussion fill, or they add a synth pad underneath what is already playing. The addition feels like the answer because the addition is something the producer can do, and doing something feels better than doing nothing, and the addition is at least guaranteed to change the energy. The change in energy is interpreted by the producer as an improvement. The producer commits the addition, moves on, reaches the next slow patch, adds another thing, and by the end of the song the arrangement has become a thicket of overlapping elements that the producer is now stuck with, because removing any of them would expose the slow patch the addition was put there to cover.

The pattern is so common that almost every song made in the bedroom-producer style of the last fifteen years has a version of it. The arrangement gets bigger as the song goes on, not because the song is building toward something, but because the producer was running away from the discomfort of slow patches and the running took the form of layering. The result is an arrangement that has more sound in it than the song actually needed and that sounds, on close listening, like a producer who was scared of empty space.

The unstated assumption inside the additive pattern is that energy in a song is the same as the number of things playing at the same time. This assumption is wrong. Energy in a song is about momentum and contrast, not about density. A song with one instrument playing a part with conviction has more energy than the same song with five instruments all doing tasteful supporting work, because the conviction is the energy and the supporting work is just paint. The producer who solves the middle section problem by adding is producing more paint and confusing it with conviction. The listener can tell the difference even when they cannot articulate what they are hearing.

The opposite of the additive pattern is not the subtractive pattern, which is just the additive pattern run backward and which has its own version of the same problem. The subtractive pattern is the producer who reaches the middle section, decides that the song is too cluttered, and starts muting things until the arrangement is bare. This usually fails too, because the subtraction is also a reflex, and the reflex is also driven by discomfort with the current state, not by a clear sense of what the song actually needs. The subtractive pattern produces middle sections that feel empty in a different way, missing the elements that were supporting the song's identity and substituting nothing for them.

The thing that actually solves the middle section problem is a different category of move, which I will call the contrast move. A contrast move is a deliberate change in one specific dimension of the song, while everything else stays where it is. The instrument that has been carrying the melody steps aside and a different instrument takes over for eight bars. The chord progression that has been looping breaks for a moment into a different progression. The drum pattern that has been steady drops out entirely for one bar and then returns. The texture stays approximately the same density, but something specific about it has changed, and the listener notices the change and is reengaged by it, because the change is information and the listener wants information.

The reason the contrast move is harder than the additive move is that the contrast move requires the producer to have a clear idea about what the song's identity actually is, so that they know which dimension to vary and which dimensions to hold constant. The additive move requires no such clarity. You can add anything to anything and feel like you have done something. The contrast move requires you to know what the song is doing in order to know what to change about it. Most producers reach for the additive move because the additive move does not require them to know what the song is doing, and most producers do not actually know what their song is doing, especially in the middle of it.

This is where the daily practice format I am working in does something specific that other workflows do not do. When you only have fifteen minutes to finish a song from an empty project, you cannot afford to be unclear about what the song is doing, because you do not have time to figure it out by trial and error. You have to make decisions about the song's identity quickly, in the first two or three minutes, and then the rest of the session is about executing on those decisions. When you reach the middle section, you already know what the song is doing, so the contrast move is available to you. The contrast move is available because the clarity is already there, and the clarity is there because the format forced you to find it early.

In the sessions on this channel, the middle sections are usually built with one of two specific contrast moves. The first move is a single-element substitution, where the instrument carrying the lead handoffs to a different instrument for a brief period and then either hands back or transforms into something else. The second move is a structural break, where one of the supporting elements drops out entirely for a few bars and the song temporarily becomes a different thing, more sparse, and then resumes with the supporting element back in place. Both of these moves are subtractive in the literal sense — they remove something — but they are not the panicked subtractive pattern I described earlier, because they are removing something with a clear purpose and the removal is the thing the listener is supposed to notice. The removal is the contrast. The contrast is the information. The information is the energy.

There is a third move that I use less often but that is the most powerful one when it works, which is the move where you change the harmonic ground under everything else without changing what is playing on top. You modulate, or you substitute one chord in the loop for a different chord, or you let the bass note drift to a different root while the upper structure stays put. The producer who can do this is using contrast at a level the listener cannot consciously identify, because the listener does not have a vocabulary for harmonic ground, but the listener feels the change exactly the same as they feel any other contrast, and the change is more powerful than any of the others because it comes from inside the song instead of from on top of it.

I am not arguing that you should never add things in middle sections. Sometimes the right move is genuinely an addition, when the song wanted that addition all along and the addition is the contrast, not the cover. I am arguing that the additive reflex is the wrong default, and that the producer who keeps reaching for the additive move when the song needs a contrast move is producing arrangements that are bigger and more cluttered and less alive than they could be. The honest middle section is the one where the producer asked, calmly, what specifically about the song the middle of the song should change, and then changed exactly that thing, and changed nothing else, and let the change do its work without panic.

Most middle sections fail because the producer was scared. The scared producer adds. The calm producer contrasts. The difference between the two is not in the tools or the talent. The difference is in the producer's relationship to the discomfort of the slow patch in the middle, and in whether they can stay with the discomfort long enough to ask what it actually wants.
