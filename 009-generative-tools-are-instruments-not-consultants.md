There is a category of music software that produces musical material on its own, by following rules that the producer has set up but does not directly control. These tools have been around for a long time, going back at least to the experiments of John Cage and the early computer music programs of the nineteen-fifties, but in the last few years they have become a normal feature of mainstream music production software. Bitwig has them. The Grid in Bitwig 6 added more of them, including the Turing Machines and the Logic Gates, which are devices that produce sequences of notes or rhythms based on probability and feedback rather than on a fixed pattern the producer drew. Most other modern music production programs have similar things now. The category has a name, which is generative music tools, and the name is unfortunate because it has caused most people to misunderstand what the tools are for.

The misunderstanding has a specific shape. The shape is the assumption that a generative tool is something you consult, the way you would consult a search engine or a recommendation algorithm. You set up the tool, you press play, the tool produces something, and you keep what you like. The producer's role is curatorial. The tool's role is creative. The relationship is roughly the same relationship a magazine editor has with the articles they receive from freelancers. The editor does not write the article. The editor reads what was written and decides whether to publish it. This framing of generative tools is so common that most of the documentation for these tools is written from inside it. The documentation tells you how to set up the parameters and listen to what comes out and adjust until the output is satisfying.

The framing is wrong, and being wrong about it costs you almost everything that makes the tools interesting. The framing is wrong because a generative tool is not a freelancer producing articles for an editor. A generative tool is an instrument. An instrument is a thing you play. The thing that distinguishes an instrument from a consultant is that an instrument responds in real time to what the player does, and the player's relationship to the instrument is one of physical and mental coupling rather than evaluation. A guitar is an instrument because the guitar's behavior is determined by what the guitarist's hands are doing in the moment, and the guitarist's hands are responding to what they hear, and the loop between the hands and the sound and the next hand movement is what playing actually is. A guitar is not a consultant. You do not strum the guitar, listen to what it produced, and decide whether to keep it.

A generative tool, set up correctly and approached as an instrument, works the same way. You play it. The parameters are the controls. You change them in real time and listen to how the output shifts. You develop a feel for how the tool responds to your changes. Over time you build the same kind of muscle memory you would build with a guitar, except the muscles are slightly different because the inputs are knobs and probability sliders instead of strings and frets. The output is not something you wait for and then judge. The output is something you are continuously shaping, in the moment, as part of an ongoing performance, the way a guitarist is continuously shaping the sound of the guitar with their fingers without ever stopping to evaluate it as a finished thing.

The opposite of the instrument framing is the consultant framing, which is what most producers default to when they encounter these tools for the first time. The consultant framing is also what the AI music generation companies are trying to scale up, except they have replaced the small-scale generative tool with a giant neural network and the producer with a person typing prompts. The structure is the same. You set parameters or write a prompt. You wait. You receive output. You judge whether to keep it. The producer in this framing has been demoted to the role of the magazine editor, evaluating someone else's work, and the someone else has been promoted to the role of the artist. The producer is no longer playing anything. The producer is consulting an oracle and grading the answers.

There is a similar but different concept that often gets mistaken for the instrument framing, which is the idea of generative tools as collaborators. Collaboration has a specific meaning that does not apply here. Collaboration involves two parties with their own intentions negotiating a shared outcome. A generative tool has no intentions. The tool is not negotiating with you. Calling it a collaborator gives it a kind of agency it does not have, and the projection of agency is part of what makes the consultant framing feel acceptable, because we think of consultants as having ideas of their own and we are willing to defer to them. An instrument has no ideas of its own. The ideas come from the player. The instrument has properties, the way a saxophone has properties, and those properties shape what the player can do, but the properties are not ideas and the saxophone is not negotiating.

What the instrument framing unlocks is the possibility of skill. You cannot become skilled at consulting an oracle. The oracle is opaque and your interaction with it is one-shot and the loop is too slow to develop any feel. You can become skilled at playing an instrument, even an instrument as strange as a Turing Machine module patched into a Grid feedback loop, because the loop is fast enough that your hands and ears can learn the relationship between input and output and start anticipating it. After a few months of playing one of these tools as an instrument you stop having to listen to what it just produced in order to know what it is going to produce next. You start having intentions, in your hands, that the tool turns into sound. That is what playing an instrument means. That is the thing the consultant framing makes impossible.

The reason the consultant framing keeps winning, despite being wrong, is that the consultant framing is easier. The instrument framing requires the producer to develop skill, which takes months. The consultant framing requires nothing. You set up the tool, you grade the output, you keep what you like. You can produce results on your first day. The results will be mediocre and they will be the same kind of mediocre that every other producer using the tool in the same way is producing, but they will be results, and most producers stop there and decide they have learned the tool. They have not. They have learned the consultant framing of the tool, and the consultant framing is the part of the tool that has nothing to do with what the tool can actually do.

In the sessions on this channel, when I am using a generative module, I am playing it. I am turning the knobs in real time, I am changing the seeds, I am routing the output to different destinations on the fly, I am responding to what I hear with my hands moving on the controls. The recording captures this because it captures everything. You can watch me playing a Turing Machine the way you would watch a guitarist playing a guitar, except the gestures are smaller and stranger and the instrument was invented forty years ago by someone who was probably not thinking about whether it counted as an instrument when they invented it. It counts as an instrument now because someone is playing it. That is the only test that matters.
