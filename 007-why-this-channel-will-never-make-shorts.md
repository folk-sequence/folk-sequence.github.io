I am working in a tradition that did not have the tools I am using. The tradition is not a single thing, but it has a few names attached to it that point at the right shape. Townes Van Zandt is one of them. John Prine is another. Son Volt is in there, as is most of what came out of the alt-country and No Depression scenes in the nineteen-nineties, and behind all of that is the older lineage of acoustic folk and country songwriting that goes back further than the question this essay is about. None of these people used a computer to make their records. Some of them worked with engineers who used computers to mix and master the tape, but the music itself was played on instruments held in human hands in rooms with microphones, and the songs were made of decisions about what to play and what to sing into the room while the tape was rolling. The computer was, at most, a tool of preservation. It was not a tool of composition.

I am making music that wants to belong to that tradition, with a producer's relationship to a computer that those musicians did not have, and the question I am inside of all the time is what the computer enables that their methods could not, and whether the things it enables are worth the things they cost. The question is not whether the computer is allowed in the tradition. The question is what specifically the computer adds, in terms of musical possibility, that an acoustic-only practice could not have produced, and whether the additions justify a worker who chooses to work this way instead of choosing the older way.

Here is the part where I have to be honest about a thing that is true for me and is also true for most producers in my situation, which is that I do not have the level of physical playing skill on a real banjo to make the records I am hearing in my head. I can play a little. I am not a beginner. But the parts I want a banjo to play in my arrangements are not the parts my hands can produce on a real banjo, and the gap between what I can imagine and what I can play is wider than the gap I want my final recordings to expose. The computer closes that gap. Inside the computer I can tell a virtual banjo to play the part I am hearing, with the timing and the articulation and the dynamics that match the part, and the part will exist in the recording as a real-sounding banjo part even though no banjo was played in the room. This is not cheating, exactly, but it is also not the same thing as what John Prine was doing when he held an actual guitar in his actual hands and sang into an actual microphone in an actual room. The two practices are different practices and I want to be honest that they are different and not pretend that the difference does not matter.

What I want to argue is that the difference, while real, is less important than it looks, and that the computer-native practice has advantages of its own that the acoustic tradition could not have produced, and that these advantages are worth taking seriously instead of writing off as the cheap shortcut they are sometimes accused of being. The first advantage is the obvious one. The computer lets a producer work in an arrangement that uses instruments the producer does not own, cannot play, and would never have the time to learn to play at the level required. This is significant because the songs that can be made with a small set of instruments the producer can play are not the same songs that can be made with a large set of instruments the producer cannot play. There are arrangements that exist in my recordings that I could not have arrived at by writing the song on an acoustic guitar first and then figuring out how to translate it. The arrangement is the song, in those cases. The arrangement was composed in the computer, with the imagined instruments doing imagined things, and the song that resulted is a song that did not exist in any acoustic precursor.

The second advantage is one I almost never see argued for, which is that working in a computer lets a producer try things and discard them at a speed that an acoustic practice cannot match. The acoustic practice has its own version of this, in the form of jamming and improvising, but the trial-and-error loop in an acoustic practice is constrained by the physical reality of the instruments and the room and the time it takes to set up a recording. The trial-and-error loop in a computer is closer to instantaneous. I can hear an idea, drop it into the project, decide in fifteen seconds whether it works, undo it, try the next thing, and repeat the loop dozens of times in the span of a single take. The acoustic practice cannot do this. The acoustic practice has to commit, listen, recommit, and the recommitment is slower than the imagining. I am not arguing that the computer's speed is always good. Sometimes the slowness of the acoustic practice is exactly the thing that produces the good result, because the slowness forces the player to commit to ideas that the speed would have let them dismiss. But the speed has its own gifts, and one of the gifts is that you can explore territory in an afternoon that would have taken a band a week of rehearsals to explore.

The third advantage is the one I am still working out and which I want to be careful about, because the obvious version of the argument is wrong. The obvious version is that the computer lets you collaborate with a generative system that can produce musical material in response to prompts, and that this is a new kind of creative partnership unavailable to musicians working with only acoustic instruments. This is true in a literal sense, but it is misleading as a description, because most of what the generative tools produce is mediocre by default, and the producer who uses them as oracles will end up with mediocre music. The actual interesting thing the generative tools can do is take over the parts of the work that are mechanical and repetitive, the parts where I would otherwise be clicking and dragging for forty minutes to set up a routine I have already decided on, so that I can spend the saved forty minutes on the parts of the work that actually require my attention. The generative tools are not substituting for my decisions. They are clearing the underbrush so I have more time to make decisions. This is the use case that justifies them. The use case that does not justify them is asking them to make the song.

I do not think the computer-native practice is better than the acoustic tradition. I do not think it is worse, either. I think it is a different practice, with different gifts and different costs, and that the producer who chooses to work this way has chosen a tradition that is still being invented in real time by the people who are willing to work in it seriously. The tradition is not yet old enough to have its Townes Van Zandt or its John Prine, the figures who would have made the case for the practice by the example of their work. The tradition is being made by producers who do not have famous names, working in genres the music industry barely knows what to do with, on tools that did not exist twenty years ago and will be replaced by different tools twenty years from now.

The audience I am making this for is not the audience that wants to learn how to use a particular piece of software, and it is not the audience that wants tutorials, and it is not the audience that wants to follow the growth of a YouTube channel as a phenomenon in itself. The audience I am making this for is producers who already know how to make music in a computer, who are working in a related tradition, who are stuck on a specific problem they have been banging their head against, and who might find in one of these sessions the one specific moment of insight that gets them past the block they are currently inside. I know this audience because I am part of it. I do not learn from books or from videos when I am cold. I learn when I have been wrestling with a problem for long enough to have the context to understand the small thing somebody else does that solves it for me. The Folk Sequence sessions exist to be that small thing for someone else, eventually, when that someone else is stuck on their own version of the same problem I happened to be working on the day I made the recording. I cannot predict which session will turn out to be the one that helps which producer. I just have to keep making them and trust that the help arrives if the work is honest and specific.

The art is the practice. The how-to is a side effect available to anyone who happens to need it. The tradition I am working in did not have a computer and could not have made my arrangements. I am working in it anyway, with the tools I have, and I am trying to add to it instead of trying to substitute for it.
