A haunting fiddle tune in G minor, warning of the perils of adult life when the government wages war as a market play. The violin is ever present in mid range, restrained.

I approached this piece with two ideas. First, have one supporting melody instrument play a different part on chord progression repeat three of four to mix it up, give it a tad of structure, make it slightly less repetitive. In fifteen minutes I have only made loops, so I am starting to explore ways that I might be able to quickly arrange something more like a song with different parts and instruments coming in and out. For now this is one simple step.

Second, take it easy on myself. The past three videos have all involved methods of generating midi notes that were innovative and un-practiced, so use the tools I know best and relax and enjoy it a bit, less anxiety over whether or not the whole thing will fall apart.

The piece sits in G minor throughout, no modulation, no escape. The fiddle carries the warning melody while supporting instruments provide the weight of the message. On the third repetition, a supporting voice shifts its pattern, a small structural gesture that says the song is paying attention to itself.

This is folk music in the oldest sense, a warning passed along through melody. The government wages war as a market play, and the adult who signs up for it signs up for a kind of death. The fiddle knows this. The fiddle has always known this.

Most producers approach arrangement as a problem of addition. What can I layer here? What counter-melody fills the gap? The better question is subtraction and variation. What changes on repeat three that tells the listener the song is alive? In this case, one instrument plays a different part, and that difference is enough to keep the fifteen-minute loop from collapsing under its own repetition.

The tools here are the ones I know best, no new tricks, no untested workflows. Sometimes the innovation is in the restraint. Sometimes the point is to make something that holds together without requiring constant intervention.

The fiddle remains. The warning remains. The government continues to wage war as a market play. The adult who enlists knows the terms and signs anyway. This is the perils of adult life. This is G minor.
