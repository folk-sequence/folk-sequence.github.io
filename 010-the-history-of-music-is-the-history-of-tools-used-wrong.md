In nineteen eighty, the Roland corporation released a drum machine called the TR-808. The TR-808 was a commercial failure. It was meant to be a tool for producers and bands to make demo recordings of songs they would later record with real drummers, and as a tool for that purpose it was not very good, because the sounds it produced did not sound like real drums. The kick drum was a low, round, almost subsonic thump that bore no resemblance to the percussive crack of a real kick drum being hit with a real beater. The snare was a thin, bright, electronic-sounding pop. The clap sounded like nothing in the natural world. Roland sold the TR-808 for three years, at a price that was more than most people wanted to pay for a tool that was supposed to be a placeholder for the real thing, and then they discontinued it and moved on. The model was, by every measure their product team would have used at the time, a flop.

Within five years of the discontinuation, the TR-808 had become the foundation of an entire genre of music. By the late nineteen-eighties it was the most important drum machine in hip-hop production. By the nineteen-nineties it was central to electronic dance music in nearly all of its subgenres. By the two thousands it was a cliche. By the twenty-tens it was on every chart-topping pop record. The kick drum that did not sound like a kick drum had become one of the most recognizable sounds in popular music, and the reason it became one of the most recognizable sounds in popular music was that producers had stopped trying to use the TR-808 as a drum-machine-for-demos and started using it as a thing in itself, with sounds that did not sound like drums and were therefore not trying to be drums and were therefore free to be something new.

This is the most important pattern in the history of music technology, and it is so consistent that I am suspicious of any tool that does not eventually go through it. The pattern is that a new tool is released for one purpose, the tool is mediocre at that purpose, the tool gets repurposed by people who do not care about the original purpose, and the repurposing turns out to be the actual reason the tool exists. The original purpose is a kind of cover story that the manufacturer needed in order to justify making the tool at all. The real purpose, which is almost always something the manufacturer did not anticipate and would not have approved, becomes obvious in retrospect.

The same pattern applies to the electric guitar, which was invented to make the guitar loud enough to be heard in big bands, and which then became the foundation of an entirely different kind of music in which loudness was the point and the big band context was abandoned. The same pattern applies to magnetic tape, which was invented for archival recording and which became, in the hands of musique concrète composers and dub producers and hip-hop sample manipulators, an instrument for cutting up and rearranging sound in ways that have nothing to do with archival. The same pattern applies to the turntable, which was invented to play records, and which became, in the hands of disco DJs and then hip-hop DJs, an instrument for playing the records themselves as percussion and pitch sources. The same pattern applies to the Auto-Tune plugin, which was invented to subtly correct out-of-tune vocal performances and which became, after T-Pain and Cher and a hundred other producers cranked the parameters past their intended range, a foundational sound of contemporary pop music.

The unstated assumption inside almost all music technology marketing is that the tools have intended uses and that the producer should respect those intended uses, and that respecting the intended uses is how you get good results. The assumption is wrong in approximately every case that has ever mattered. The producers who got good results with the TR-808 were the producers who ignored the manual. The producers who got good results with magnetic tape were the producers who took the tape off the deck and cut it with scissors. The producers who got good results with Auto-Tune were the producers who turned the retune speed all the way up and let the artifacts become the sound.

The opposite of using a tool as intended is not using the tool wrong. Using a tool wrong implies that there is a right way and a wrong way, and that the right way is the manufacturer's way. The opposite of using a tool as intended is treating the tool as a thing in itself, with properties you can investigate and use without reference to what it was supposed to be for. A drum machine has properties. Some of those properties are the intended properties, the ones the manufacturer designed in on purpose, and some of them are unintended properties, the ones that emerged from the technical implementation and that the manufacturer did not notice or did not care about. The unintended properties are usually where the interesting work happens, because the unintended properties have not been smoothed over by a design committee and have not been optimized for the average use case and are therefore weirder and more specific and more likely to produce something that sounds like itself instead of like everything else.

There is a similar idea that sounds correct but is actually different, which is the idea that you should be experimental and play around with your tools. The experimental framing is friendly and the experimental framing is encouraged in beginner tutorials, and the experimental framing usually does not lead anywhere because it is too unfocused. Random experimentation produces random results. The thing I am describing is not random experimentation. It is something more specific, which is the deliberate decision to look for the properties of a tool that the manufacturer did not advertise, and to use those specific properties as the foundation of a piece of work, and to do this not as an experiment but as a strategy. The TR-808 producers were not experimenting. They knew exactly what they were doing. They had decided that the tool's failure to sound like real drums was its most interesting property, and they built whole records around that property.

The principle generalizes beyond music technology. Every tool that produces interesting work in any field is being used wrong by someone. The wrong usage is not a bug. The wrong usage is what tools are for, in the long run. The intended uses are the cover story that gets the tool funded and built and shipped. The unintended uses are why the tool ends up mattering. If you are using a tool only in the way the manual describes, you are using the cover story. The actual tool is something else.

In the sessions on this channel I am sometimes using Bitwig as Bitwig was designed to be used, and I am sometimes using it in ways that I am pretty sure the people who built Bitwig did not have in mind. Both modes produce interesting work. The second mode produces more of the interesting work than the first mode, in roughly the proportion you would expect from the history I just laid out. Which means most of what I am trying to do is figure out, for the specific tools I am using, what the unintended properties are, and then build around them.
