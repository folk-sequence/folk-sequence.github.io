The instinct most musicians have, especially the careful ones, is that a song should be released when it is ready. The problem with this instinct is that ready is not actually a property of the song. It is a property of the musician's relationship to the song. A song is ready when the person making it can let go of it, and most people, most of the time, cannot let go.

This is not the same as perfectionism, though the two overlap and are easy to confuse. Perfectionism is the belief that the song could be objectively better with more work, that there is some improved version of it waiting on the other side of more effort. The thing I am describing is something stranger and more universal. It is the belief that the song could be safer with more work. The unfinished song carries no risk because nobody has heard it yet. The released song carries every kind of risk a song can carry. So the careful musician finds reasons to keep the song unfinished, and the reasons are always real and reasonable, and the song stays in the project folder for years while the musician convinces themselves they are still working on it.

Daily practice is the antidote because it removes the option to keep the song safe. If the song has to be done by the end of the session, the part of the brain that says not yet does not get to vote. The decision is made by the clock instead of by the careful musician's risk calculation, and the result is that the song gets committed to even when committing means publishing something that another week of effort would have improved. This sounds reckless, and it is, in the same way that any good practice has a reckless quality at its core. You are deliberately giving up the option to be safe.

The opposite of daily practice is not weekly practice or monthly practice, which are just slower versions of the same idea. The opposite of daily practice is conditional practice, which is what most musicians actually do even when they think they are practicing daily. Conditional practice is the discipline of working when the conditions are right. You sit down when the inspiration is good. You commit when the song is ready. You publish when the timing makes sense. The conditions are never quite right enough for the careful musician, so the practice happens in fits and starts and the songs accumulate as half-finished sketches in folders the musician stops opening.

What daily practice produces, at least at first, is not better songs. What it produces is a different relationship between the musician and the work. The work stops being precious because it cannot afford to be. The work becomes something that gets made because it is the time to make it, not because the moment has arrived. This sounds joyless when described, the kind of grim discipline you associate with people who write self-help books about productivity. In practice it is the opposite. The pressure of the deadline takes the deliberation away, and the deliberation was the part that was making the work miserable in the first place. You stop second-guessing the bassline because there is no time to second-guess it, and after a few weeks of not second-guessing things you discover that your first instincts are usually fine, and after a few months you stop being able to remember what the second-guessing was even for.

There is a related discipline on this channel, which is the refusal to edit. The Folk Sequence videos are screen recordings of complete sessions, mistakes included, dead air included, the long stretches where I sit there not playing anything because I am thinking about what to do next. If I cut the dead air the recording would be a kind of lie about how songs actually get made. Songs get made by sitting there making mistakes and second-guessing yourself for a long time, and then occasionally doing something good, and then sitting there again. The mistakes and the dead air are not a flaw in the recording. They are the recording. Removing them would be the same cowardice that keeps half-finished songs in the project folder for years, dressed up as editorial restraint.

A bad daily session is still a daily session. The bar is not producing something good. The bar is showing up and finishing. If the bar were producing something good I would be back where I started, staring at half-finished folders, telling myself I would come back to them when the moment was right, knowing that the moment was never going to come because the moment is never going to come, because the moment is the part you make up to give yourself permission to keep waiting.

The right moment is the moment when you decide that this is the moment. There is no other kind.
