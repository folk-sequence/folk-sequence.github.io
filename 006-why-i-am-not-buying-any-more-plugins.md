I want to tell you about how I found the tools I use, because the story is not the story most plugin essays tell. The story most plugin essays tell is about restraint, or about the wisdom of working with less, or about how the producers with the smallest toolboxes have the most distinctive sounds. I am about to tell you something different and more honest, which is that I have spent something close to ten thousand dollars on plugins over the last decade, that I am broke as a result of this, and that I do not regret the spending in the way the standard essay would have me regret it. I regret the credit cards. I do not regret the hunt.

Here is the situation that produced the hunt. I make music that has, at its center, acoustic instruments from the folk and old country traditions. Acoustic guitar fingerstyle. Banjo, the kind that does not roll like bluegrass but plays clawhammer or melodic phrases that sit inside an arrangement instead of leading it. Mandolin, the instrument that always sounds wrong when you fake it. The kind of music I am making asks for those instruments specifically, in arrangements that also include drums and electronic elements, and I am making it inside a music production program where the instruments themselves are not in the room. I cannot mic up my own banjo, partly because I do not have the level of physical playing skill on a real banjo to produce the parts I am hearing in my head, and partly because the workflow of recording acoustic instruments at the speed of daily practice would not survive a single week. I needed the instruments to live inside the software, played by me from a keyboard or generated from MIDI, and I needed them to sound like the actual instruments and not like the pad-with-some-plucked-attack approximations that most virtual instrument libraries pretend are acoustic banjos.

If you have never gone looking for a virtual banjo that responds well to a music production environment, I cannot easily convey how poorly served this need is by the market. The major sample library companies, the ones with the marketing budgets and the YouTube sponsorship deals, have orchestral strings and pianos and synth pads and the obvious electric guitars and basses. Their folk instrument offerings, when they exist, are usually shallow, sampled at one velocity, with two or three articulations, designed to be a flavor element you add to a track that is mostly something else. They are not designed to carry a song. The producer who needs to carry a song with a banjo is not the producer the major libraries are building for. You have to go further down the long tail, into smaller companies, into specialty libraries, into corners of the market where the marketing budgets are zero and the only way to find the good stuff is to try things and listen.

I tried things and I listened, for years, and the list of what I now own was assembled almost entirely by trial and error, paid for with credit card minimum payments and the kind of optimism that has cost me money in many other areas of my life as well. Some of the things I bought were bad. Some of them were fine for one specific job and not for anything else. Some of them are now permanent fixtures in everything I make. The ones that are permanent fixtures are not the ones I would have predicted would be permanent fixtures when I was buying them. There is no way I could have known in advance which ones would survive the test of being used week after week for a year. The only way to know was to use them.

The acoustic guitar problem I solved with Native Instruments Session Guitarist Picked Acoustic, and the electric guitar problem in the same family I solved with Session Guitarist Sunburst, both run in their melody mode, which is the part of the plugin that lets you actually play melodic lines instead of forcing you into a strummed-chord workflow. I had to discover that the melody mode existed by reading the manual after I had already owned the plugins for a year and assumed they were only good for chord progressions. The banjo problem I solved with the Ample Sound Ethno Ensemble, which contains a banjo that responds well to MIDI velocity and articulation in a way that almost no other sample library does, and which I found through a forum post from a person who was working in a related genre and had already done the hunting on my behalf. The mandolin problem I solved with the 8Dio Mandolin, which was on sale at the time and which I bought on a hope that turned out to be justified. None of these were the obvious choices. None of them showed up in the typical "best virtual instruments" listicles, because the listicles are written for producers in genres the listicle writers actually understand, and folk is not one of those genres.

For the electric guitar tone I was after, which is the specific kind of warm and slightly broken sound that Son Volt and the late Jay Farrar records have, I needed an amp simulator that did not aim at metal or modern pop, and I went through about six of them before I landed on Waves CLA Guitars, which has a setting that gets close enough to the Son Volt sound that I can tweak it the rest of the way by ear. I cannot tell you how many hours I spent listening to demos and reading forum threads and downloading trials before I knew Waves CLA Guitars was the answer. I can tell you that the only way I found it was by spending the money to actually try things, and that no amount of reading would have substituted for putting the plugin in a session and playing through it.

The MIDI side of the sessions has its own version of this story. I needed plugins that could generate musical material in response to a few inputs, that could talk to each other across tracks so that a chord progression on one track could drive an arpeggiator on another track without me having to manually copy notes between them. I found Pilot Plugins, which is a small company making note generators and processors with a simple, powerful interface, and which solved problems I had been working around for years with inelegant workarounds. The Pilot Plugins suite is the kind of thing nobody is going to tell you about, because nobody is doing influencer marketing for it, and the only way I found it was by being deep enough into the hunt to follow obscure links from obscure forum posts. Sugar Bytes DrumComputer is another one of these. It is a drum machine plugin with an auto-fill feature that, the first time it generated a fill that I would have spent twenty minutes programming by hand, made me feel like I had found something other producers in my genre would also need to know about, and that I had been paying for the privilege of finding it.

I am hands-on. I learn by doing. I do not learn from watching tutorials about plugins and I do not learn from reading reviews and I do not learn from asking other producers what they use, because what I would learn from those sources is what the average producer in a different genre uses for a different purpose. The hunt was the only education available to me. The hunt happened in real time, with my hands on the controls, listening to the plugin in my actual session with my actual material, deciding whether the thing I had just bought was going to live in my workflow or get demoted to the folder of things I do not open anymore. This is expensive, and slow, and the only way it gets faster is if somebody else has already done the hunt for the exact niche you are working in and is willing to tell you what they found, and almost nobody does this, because almost nobody is working in your exact niche.

A plugin is like a pair of running shoes. You cannot judge it from the demo. You cannot judge it from the spec sheet. You cannot judge it from the reviews. You have to put miles on it. The miles take time and they take projects and they take the willingness to use the thing you bought even when you suspect it might not be the right thing. The plugins that turned out to be the right thing for me are the ones I happened to keep using past the suspicion. The ones I stopped using are the ones the suspicion turned out to be right about. I had to do the running to find out which was which, and the running cost money on top of the cost of the shoes.

The thing I would say to a producer just starting out, if I could only say one thing, is that the hunt is real and the hunt is expensive and there is no shortcut, but you should not put it on credit cards. You should pay for it as you go. You should buy one plugin, use it for six months, sell it on the secondhand market if it does not work out, and only then buy the next one. I did not do this. I bought new plugins on top of the ones I had not finished evaluating yet, because I was excited and impatient and because the marketing emails always made the next thing sound like the answer, and the result of doing it that way is that I owe money I cannot pay and that I will probably be paying interest on for years. The hunt I did was necessary. The financing I used to do it was not.

I am still in the middle of this. I cannot afford to buy anything new and that has turned out to be a good thing for my work, because it forces me to use the tools I already have and to develop a real relationship with them. The relationship is finally happening, three years into the buying freeze, and it is happening with the tools I would have eventually arrived at if I had been more patient with the original hunt. The Native Instruments Session Guitarist plugins. The Ample Sound banjo. The 8Dio mandolin. The Waves CLA Guitars. The Pilot Plugins note generators. The Sugar Bytes drum machine. These are the tools the daily practice on this channel is built on, and they are the tools the hunt produced, and I would not trade them for the money I spent finding them, even though I would trade the way I paid for them in a heartbeat.

If you are about to start your own hunt, I cannot tell you which plugins to buy, because the answer is specific to whatever niche you are working in and you will have to find your own answers the same way I had to find mine. What I can tell you is that the hunt is the work, that the hunt is going to take longer than you want it to, that the things you are looking for are probably not on the front page of any plugin store, and that the only way through is to use what you buy long enough to know whether it deserves to stay. The shoes need miles. The miles take time. The time is the price. Pay it slowly if you can.
