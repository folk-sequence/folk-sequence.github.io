There is a particular kind of advice that gets handed out to new music producers, and it always comes in the form of rules. Mix in mono to check your stereo image. Cut everything below eighty hertz on every track that is not a kick or a bass. Use sidechain compression on the pads when the kick hits. Bus the drums together. Reference your mixes against commercial releases. Leave headroom on the master. The rules are usually correct, in the sense that following them will produce a result that sounds professionally competent, and they are usually wrong, in the sense that following them will not produce a result that sounds like anything in particular.

The interesting question is not whether the rules are right. The interesting question is what the rules are actually for, and what they are protecting you from, and what they cost you when you follow them without thinking about them.

The rules exist because they encode the accumulated experience of professional engineers working in commercial studios making records intended for radio. Every rule is the answer to a problem that came up over and over again in that specific context. Cut everything below eighty hertz, because if you do not, the low end gets muddy and the record will not translate to small speakers, and small speakers are how most people hear records on the radio. Mix in mono, because if you do not, your stereo separation might collapse on a phone speaker. Reference against commercial releases, because if you do not, your mix will be quieter than the records it sits next to in a playlist. The rules are real solutions to real problems, and the real problems are the problems of making radio-ready records in commercial studios.

The trouble is that most people making music now are not making radio-ready records in commercial studios. They are making records to be heard on streaming services through bluetooth speakers, or in headphones, or as background music while someone scrolls. The rules were optimized for a context that has only partial overlap with the context most people are actually working in, and the parts of the context that have changed are the parts that the rules were quietly assuming.

Here is what gets lost when you follow the rules without thinking. The rules push everything toward a specific shape, which is the shape of a competent commercial record from somewhere between nineteen ninety-five and two thousand and ten. The frequency balance is the same shape. The dynamic range is the same shape. The stereo field is the same shape. The compression curves are the same shape. Records that follow the rules sound like they belong to a particular family, and the family is large enough that you can sit inside it and not notice you are inside it. You have to leave the family on purpose to hear how distinctive its accent is.

The opposite of following the rules is not breaking them randomly. Breaking the rules randomly is what new producers do when they get told that the rules are not absolute, and the result usually sounds like a mess, because the rules really were solving real problems and ignoring the problems does not make them go away. The opposite of following the rules is something more specific, which is understanding what each rule is solving for and then asking, in your particular case, whether that problem is one you have, and if not, whether the rule is costing you something you would rather keep.

Most of the rules are solving for translation. Translation is the audio engineer's word for whether the mix sounds the same on different playback systems. A mix that translates well sounds approximately correct on big speakers, small speakers, headphones, car stereos, and phones. The rules about cutting low frequencies, mixing in mono, and avoiding extreme stereo placement are mostly translation rules. They exist because in the commercial context you do not know how the listener will hear your record, and you want it to be at least acceptable in all the places they might play it.

But translation is a tradeoff. The mix that translates perfectly to every system is the mix that has been sanded smooth to the point where it cannot be distinctive on any specific system. The records I love most are usually the ones that sound great on one specific kind of playback, often headphones, and that the producer optimized for that kind of playback at the cost of how they sound somewhere else. The opening of My Bloody Valentine's Loveless does not translate well. It is not supposed to. It is supposed to overwhelm you on headphones, which is where the producer expected you to hear it, and the choice to go all the way in that direction is what makes the record sound like itself.

The thing I do on this channel is screen recording of full sessions in Bitwig with no edits and no narration, so the rules I am navigating are not the same rules a finished-record producer is navigating. Some of them still apply. I still want the low end to be defined enough that you can hear it on a laptop speaker. I still care about how things translate.

What is different is how I am handling the loudness rule. The conventional approach is to mix carefully, leave headroom on the master, and then run a mastering pass at the end that shapes the dynamics and lands the integrated level on whatever the target spec is. Two phases, two jobs, two mental modes. I have replaced the first half of that workflow with a real-time master chain that is doing the musical work of mastering continuously, while I play, in a way that is genuinely adaptive. The chain handles enormous swings in input level — a single kick drum hit alone in the room and a full arrangement with twenty tracks playing both come out sounding pleasant, without me touching anything in between. The chain is making decisions about dynamics and tone in response to whatever I just played, which means the musical reality of the mastering is happening at the same time as the composition. I am hearing what the mastered version sounds like while I am still deciding what to put in it.

The second half of the conventional workflow, the part that nails the integrated level to a specific target like fourteen LUFS for YouTube, is still happening. It happens in the transcode step that prepares the file for upload, where the level gets locked in with precision. That part is just spec compliance and it does not need to be a creative phase. The musical work has already been done by the master chain in the room.

This split matters because most of the mixing rules I was taught are downstream of the assumption that mastering is one thing that happens once at the end. Once mastering is happening continuously, while you compose, the rules that were protecting you from making decisions you would regret in the mastering pass start to look different. You stop leaving headroom in the conventional way because the master chain is using the headroom in real time. You stop bussing the drums into a particular standard configuration because the chain is responding to the drums as part of the whole mix, not as a separate group that needs special treatment. You stop putting the kick at a fixed loudness target because the chain will adapt around whatever you do. The rules were not wrong. They were just answers to a question I am no longer asking.

The point is not that I am breaking the rules. The point is that I asked, for each rule, what it was for, and whether the thing it was for was a thing I needed, and I kept the rules whose answer was yes and dropped the rules whose answer was no. The result is something that sounds different from a commercial record, in some places by accident and in some places on purpose, and I prefer it that way. It sounds like the room it was made in. The rules, followed without question, would have erased the room.

The advice I would give a new producer is not to break the rules and it is not to follow the rules. The advice is to find out what each rule is solving and then decide, on a case by case basis, whether you have the problem the rule is solving. Most of the time you will, and you will follow the rule. Some of the time you will not, and the rule will be costing you something, and you will let it go. The producers I admire the most are not the ones who broke the most rules. They are the ones who knew which rules they were breaking and why.
