Most music production programs ask you to pick a tempo before you start. The empty project opens with a number in a box at the top of the screen, usually one hundred twenty beats per minute by default, and the number is the first thing the program wants you to change. The convention is so old and so universal that almost nobody questions it. You pick the tempo. The tempo applies to the whole song. The grid of the project arranges itself around the tempo, the metronome ticks at the tempo, the loops you import get warped to the tempo, the program assumes that the tempo is a setting that the song lives inside of and that the setting is a thing you decide once at the beginning and rarely touch again.

I want to argue that this convention is doing more harm to most producers' work than the producers realize, and that the way it is doing harm is by promoting tempo from a musical decision into a technical configuration. The harm is invisible because nothing about the workflow points at it. The program is not telling you that you have made a mistake. The program is just letting the assumption sit in the background, where it shapes every choice you make about the song without ever being examined.

Here is the thing the convention is hiding. Tempo is one of the most expressive parameters in music. The difference between a song at seventy beats per minute and the same song at one hundred and twenty beats per minute is not a small difference. It is the difference between a ballad and a dance track. It is the difference between a song that lets the listener sit with each chord and a song that hurries them along to the next one. It is the difference between a vocal that can breathe and a vocal that has to fit. The choice of tempo determines what the song can be, what it can feel like, what it can mean. A tempo decision is at the same level of importance as a chord progression decision, or a melodic decision, or a decision about which instrument carries the main idea. It deserves the same kind of attention.

The convention treats tempo as if it were at a different level. The convention treats tempo as a setting, like the sample rate or the bit depth or the time signature, something you configure once when you open the project and then forget about. The producer fills in the box with one hundred twenty because the box wants a number and one hundred twenty is the safe answer, and then they spend the next two hours making decisions about chords and melodies and instruments and arrangements without ever going back to the box and asking whether one hundred twenty was actually the right tempo for the song they ended up writing. By the time the song is done, the tempo has become a fact about the song instead of a choice the producer made, and the producer has forgotten that the choice was even available.

The opposite of treating tempo as a setting is not changing the tempo every bar. The opposite of treating tempo as a setting is treating it as a musical parameter that you compose with, the way you compose with chords and melodies. Composing with tempo means picking it on purpose, with reasons, in relation to what the song is trying to do, and being willing to change it if the song wants something different than you thought it wanted at the beginning. It also means being willing to use multiple tempos in a single song when the song requires it, which most producers think is forbidden but which has a long history in actual music, going back centuries, and which the program is perfectly capable of supporting if you ask it to.

There is a similar idea that often gets confused with this one, which is the idea that you should be flexible about tempo and tweak it until the song feels right. The flexibility framing sounds like the same argument I am making but it is not. The flexibility framing assumes that tempo is still fundamentally a setting, just one that you fiddle with more than other settings. The argument I am making is stronger. The argument is that tempo is a compositional element on the same level as melody and chord, that the producer should be making tempo decisions throughout the writing process, and that those decisions should be motivated by the same things that motivate any other compositional decision, which is the producer's sense of what the song is trying to be.

In the sessions on this channel I am usually starting with a tempo I have picked on purpose. Sometimes the tempo comes first, before any of the other ideas, because the tempo is what the song is most fundamentally about. A song at sixty beats per minute is asking different questions than a song at one hundred and forty, and those questions shape every choice that follows. Sometimes I pick a tempo and then change my mind ten minutes into the session, when the song reveals that it wants to move slower or faster than I thought, and the change of mind is itself a creative event. The change of mind costs me in the workflow because the program has been operating on the assumption that the tempo was settled, and undoing that assumption requires some manual work, but the cost is worth paying, because the alternative is a song stuck at the wrong tempo because the wrong tempo was the first one I typed into the box.

There is a use of tempo that almost nobody talks about, which is tempo as a way of revealing the structure of a song to the listener. A song that gradually slows over its last minute is telling the listener something specific about how the song wants to end, and the something is impossible to communicate any other way. A song that briefly speeds up at the moment of its emotional peak is using the speed change as a kind of exclamation mark, and the exclamation mark would be unavailable if the tempo were locked. The classical tradition has been doing this for hundreds of years, with terms like accelerando and rallentando that describe these gestures explicitly and that classical performers spend years learning how to execute. The pop and electronic traditions have mostly forgotten that these are options, because the click track and the grid and the configuration-box approach to tempo have made them feel like something you would only do if you had a real reason. The truth is that the real reason is everywhere, in almost every song, and the click track has been hiding it.

I am not arguing that you should put tempo changes into every song. Most songs are best at a single tempo and it would be ridiculous to manufacture changes for the sake of having them. What I am arguing is that the choice should be a choice, not a default. Every song should have a tempo because the producer decided that that tempo was the right one for that song, not because the tempo was the number that happened to be in the box when the project opened. And when the song wants a tempo change, the producer should know that the change is available, instead of pretending that it is not.

The deeper point is that almost every parameter in a music production program is a decision the producer is allowed to make, and the program is mostly designed to hide which decisions are available, by promoting some of them to the foreground and letting the others sit quietly in configuration menus. The producer who reaches the most distinctive work is usually the producer who has gone looking for the hidden decisions and started making them on purpose. Tempo is the most obvious one because it is sitting at the top of the screen, but the same principle applies to every other thing the program lets you configure once and forget. Configuration is the program's way of letting you not decide. Composition is the producer's way of deciding anyway.
