Bitwig Studio 6 came out on March 11. Bitwig is the music-making program I record every Folk Sequence session in. The new version adds dozens of features, all of which are useful and most of which I am using. The reviews and the launch videos talked about the headline ones, the new sound-shaping modules and the new way to draw connections between things on screen, and they were right to talk about those. The feature I want to write about did not get the same attention. It looks small in the changelog. It is not small. It changes what one specific thing in music production is allowed to be, and the implications take a few minutes to work out in your head.

The feature is automation clips. To explain why automation clips matter, I have to explain what automation has always been, and to explain that I have to back up to what music production software has always tried to do.

Music production software lets you arrange sounds along a timeline and play them back. Most of the work happens in clips, which are little containers for a bit of music. A clip might hold a four-bar drum pattern, or a bassline, or a vocal take. The clips are the building blocks. You arrange them on tracks, you copy and reuse them, you give them names, you build a song out of them. The clip is the basic unit of musical material in this kind of program, and it has been since the late nineteen-nineties when these programs first looked the way they look now.

Automation is the other thing that happens. Automation is what you do when you want a parameter to move over time. The volume getting louder, the filter slowly opening, the reverb breathing in and out, the panning swaying back and forth. You draw a curve, and the curve tells the program how the parameter should change. Every program in this category has supported automation for as long as it has supported clips, which is to say forever.

The thing that nobody questioned for twenty-five years is that automation lived inside the clip. If you drew a filter sweep on a synth track, the curve was welded to the clip you drew it on. The curve and the clip were the same object as far as the program was concerned. If you wanted to use the same sweep on a different track, or in a different song, or with a different sound, you had to draw it again from memory, and the redraw was never quite the same as the original because hands are not consistent. Automation could be edited, but it could not be moved, and it could not be reused, and it could not have an identity of its own.

Automation clips break that weld. A curve in Bitwig 6 can now be its own object. You make the curve once, you save it, you drop it onto whatever you want to drop it onto. The curve becomes a building block in the same way that a drum pattern is a building block. You can keep a library of curves the way you keep a library of patterns. You can layer them. You can route the same curve to three different parameters at the same time and tweak the routing without touching the curve. The curve and the parameter it controls are no longer the same object.

This sounds at first like a workflow improvement, the kind of thing you would mention in passing in a release video. It is more than a workflow improvement. It is a shift in what automation is for in the first place. The way to see this is to look at the difference between two ideas that often get confused, modulation and motif.

Modulation is the thing you do to make a static sound less static. The filter slowly opens, the resonance creeps up, the reverb size breathes in and out. Modulation is in the service of something else. It is in the background, and it exists to support whatever the foreground is doing. A modulation curve drawn into a clip is throwaway by design, in the same way that the equalizer settings on a single track are throwaway. You do not name your modulation curves. You do not save them in a library. You do not think of them as having identity, because they are part of the texture of one specific clip and nothing else.

A motif is a different category of thing entirely. A motif is something the listener experiences as part of the song's identity. A bassline can be a motif. A vocal melody can be a motif. A drum pattern with a particular rhythm can be a motif. The defining property of a motif is that it has an identity outside of any single performance of it. You can move it to a different part of the song. You can vary it. You can quote it three minutes later in a transformed shape and the listener will recognize the reference. The motif belongs to the song the way a character belongs to a novel.

Automation has historically not been allowed to be a motif. It has been welded to its host clip, and therefore could not move, could not be quoted, could not have an identity outside of one specific bar of one specific track. The careful producers among us would sometimes do things that looked like automation motifs, by carefully redrawing the same curve in multiple places and trusting our hands to be consistent. The result was always slightly off, because hands are not consistent, and the slight inconsistencies meant the curve was not really the same curve, just a family resemblance.

Automation clips remove the constraint. A filter sweep can now be a motif in the same sense that a bassline is a motif. A volume curve can be a phrase you reuse and develop over the course of a song or across multiple songs. A modulator output can be performed across the full length of a track the way a melody is performed. The slow breathing change that makes a track feel alive can be the exact same slow breathing change you used six tracks ago, recognizable as part of your signature, building up as a personal vocabulary across an entire body of work.

This is why I called it a category change instead of a workflow improvement. The opposite of an automation clip is not the old style of automation that lived inside the clip, which is just a less convenient version of the same idea. The opposite of an automation clip is the assumption that modulation is supposed to be invisible furniture, that it exists only to make individual sounds less boring, that it is fundamentally different from the kind of musical material a listener can recognize. Bitwig 6 is letting you compose with the things that used to be furniture, and the moment you start doing that, the furniture stops looking like furniture.

In the sessions on this channel I am using automation clips for the long, slow, structural changes that hold a track together over its full duration. The gradual filter opens. The long crescendos that take a full minute to develop. The modulation that the listener does not consciously notice but that makes everything feel like it is going somewhere instead of just looping. Before Bitwig 6 those would have been redrawn for every track, fresh every time, throwaway by default. Now they are a vocabulary I am building across all of the songs on this channel, episode by episode, and after a hundred episodes the vocabulary will be a real thing with its own internal logic.

If you are on Bitwig 5, the upgrade pays for itself in the first session you do with the new features. If you are using a different program, this is the kind of capability you are missing and may not realize you are missing, because the version of you that did not know automation could be a motif did not have a way to want it.
