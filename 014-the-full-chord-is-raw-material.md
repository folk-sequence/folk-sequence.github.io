The full chord is raw material

Folk Sequence 014 — a folktronica and folk music production session in Bitwig Studio 6

Most producers think of a chord as a finished musical object. You voice it, you play it, it lands in the arrangement and does its job. The chord is the destination. But the more interesting move, and the one that separates groove-oriented production from mere composition, is to treat the chord as raw material — something you hand to the machine whole, knowing the machine is going to take it apart.

That is what this session is built on. The starting point is full bar chords in G minor, played straight, nothing fancy about the voicing or the rhythm. Just the harmony, laid down as a block. What turns those blocks into music is what happens after the chord is played. Bitwig's Note FX chain sits between the chord and the instrument, and its job is decomposition. The Arpeggiator cycles through the notes of the chord in patterns the hand would not naturally play. Other Note devices chop the timing, shift the velocity, introduce the micro-variations that make a sequence feel like it was performed rather than programmed. The chord goes in as a slab. It comes out as a groove.

This is a fundamentally different relationship with harmony than the one most DAW workflows encourage. The standard workflow is to program each note individually in the piano roll, choosing its position and duration and velocity with the mouse. That workflow treats the sequence as the product of deliberate placement. The Note FX workflow treats the sequence as the product of a system acting on simple input. The distinction matters because the second approach produces rhythmic relationships that a human hand would not choose but that a human ear immediately recognizes as interesting. The slight unevenness, the unexpected accent on the offbeat of beat three, the way a note gets swallowed by the arpeggiator's pattern and creates a gap that functions as a rest — none of that is programmed. It emerges.

The guitars in this session are M-Tron tape samples, which adds a layer that is easy to miss if you are not paying attention to what tape does to sustained sound. A real Mellotron, the original hardware, played back magnetic tape strips when you pressed a key. Each strip was a recording of a real instrument — in this case guitars — and each strip was about eight seconds long. When the tape ran out, the sound stopped. GForce's M-Tron preserves that behavior faithfully, including the wow and flutter of the tape transport, the slight pitch drift, the way the attack of each note carries the mechanical signature of a tape head engaging. The guitars are not clean samples. They are recordings of recordings, and the artifacts of that chain are the entire point.

What tape artifacts do to a chord-chopping workflow is subtle but critical. Each note in the decomposed chord inherits a slightly different tape character depending on where in the tape strip it lands. A note triggered early in the strip has a different texture than one triggered late. The flutter modulates pitch by fractions of a cent in ways that do not repeat. So when the Arpeggiator cycles through G minor and the M-Tron plays back each note, the timbre is never quite the same twice. The groove is not just rhythmic. It is timbral. The tape is doing work that no amount of piano-roll editing could replicate, because the variation is coming from a physical process that was captured decades ago in a studio in Birmingham and has been sitting on magnetic oxide ever since.

The drums are Sugarbytes DrumComputer, which is the right tool for this session because DrumComputer is a synthesizer pretending to be a drum machine. Every sound in it is generated from scratch — resonators, wavetables, resynthesis — rather than played back from a sample. The AutoFill feature is the one doing the heavy lifting here. You program a basic pattern and AutoFill creates variations by rearranging triggers, inserting rolls, adding pitch bends at transition points. The fills it generates are not random. They are structurally aware, landing at musically logical positions, and the pitch bends it introduces give the fills a liquid quality that sample-based fills almost never achieve. A sample-based fill is a sequence of discrete hits. A DrumComputer fill is a continuous gesture that happens to be made of hits.

Alongside the DrumComputer are multiple Bitwig Drum Machine instances loaded with stock presets. This is a deliberate layering strategy. The DrumComputer handles the synthetic, fluid drum voice. The Drum Machine presets handle the acoustic-leaning textures — the samples that ground the kit in something recognizable. Neither layer alone would be enough. The synthetic drums without the acoustic grounding sound like electronica. The acoustic presets without the synthetic layer sound like a demo. Together, at 112 BPM in four-four time, they create the specific kind of groove this session needs: heavy enough to nod to, subdued enough to leave room for the guitars to breathe.

The Pilot Plugins are in the chain as well, doing what they do best, which is turning a single knob into a stream of MIDI. Pilot's interface is aggressively simple — you twist a knob and notes come out, shaped by the plugin's internal logic into patterns that are musically coherent without being predictable. The value of Pilot in this context is speed. When the session is rolling and you need a bassline or an arpeggio variation and you do not want to stop and program one, Pilot generates candidates faster than your conscious mind can evaluate them. You twist, you listen, you keep or you move on. The speed matters because the session is a continuous take. There is no going back. The decision has to be made in real time, and a tool that narrows the search space from infinite to manageable is the difference between momentum and stalling.

The thing that ties all of this together — the chopped chords, the tape guitars, the synthetic fills, the Pilot-generated patterns — is that none of these elements are programmed in the traditional sense. They are all the output of systems acting on simple inputs. A held chord becomes an arpeggio. A basic drum pattern becomes a fill. A knob position becomes a melody. The producer's job in this workflow is not to place every note. It is to set up the systems, feed them the right raw material, and then make real-time decisions about which outputs to keep. The composition is happening at the system level, not the note level.

There is a moment at the very end of the session that demonstrates why this approach works. A guitar lead comes in, riding on top of everything that has been built, and it is dripping with ghost notes and delay. Ghost notes are the notes you almost do not play — the ones where your finger barely touches the string, producing a percussive thud instead of a pitched tone. In guitar playing, ghost notes are what separate a groove player from someone who just knows the right notes. They are the negative space, the notes that exist to define the shape of the notes around them. Delay takes those ghost notes and repeats them, turning each barely-there articulation into a rhythmic event that interacts with the groove the systems have already built. The lead does not fight the existing texture. It threads through it, finding the gaps that the arpeggiator and the tape flutter and the auto fills have created, and filling them with something that could only come from a human hand making real-time decisions about touch and pressure and timing.

That last moment is the argument for the whole approach. You build the groove with systems. You build the systems from simple inputs. And then, when the texture is rich enough and the pocket is deep enough, you play into it with your hands. The systems create the context. The human creates the meaning. Neither could do the other's job. The bar chord that started the whole chain was raw material, and everything that happened to it — the decomposition, the tape artifacts, the fills, the delay — was the process of turning that raw material into a place where a guitar lead with ghost notes could land and feel like it belonged there all along.
