The lyrics came to me soon after I awoke this morning. That is the best kind of lyric — the kind that arrives fully formed, before the critical mind has a chance to intervene. Even though relationships cause us pain, it would be worse to live without them in my life. Many people live that way. They build walls instead of bridges. They choose safety over connection. The song is about refusing that choice.

There is a little Merle Hazard influence here. Not Merle Haggard — Merle Hazard. Simple talk, a bit coy, a bit of a wink. Hazard's songs operate in a different register than traditional country. They are playful without being glib, sincere without being earnest. That is the tone I am after.

I do not know if I will ever put it all together into a single fifteen-minute Folk Sequence episode. But I am moving around the space of music production. This one includes arrangement work — vocals coming in and out, leaving space for other instruments to speak. During the breaks, an organ plays lead. That lead is produced by a sustained organ passed through a filter with an LFO sidechained to the vocals. The vocals are muted but the "pre" signal triggers the sidechain. This makes the organ pulse in response to the vocal presence even when the vocal is not audible. Plus a gate to make the organ more like a melody instrument that punches in and out, less wishy-washy about its purpose.

While the vocals are playing, there is aggressive sidechain compression on the "post" signal only. This keeps the organ melody from competing with the vocals. The organ ducks when the voice speaks, rises when the voice rests. It is a conversation, not a competition.

I use Scaler 3 for the chords and for the bassline. Scaler is often dismissed as a crutch for producers who do not know music theory. That is the wrong framing. Scaler is a collaborator that suggests harmonic paths the hand might not find. The bassline it generated here is more interesting than anything I would have written manually. The point is not whether a human or a machine originated the idea. The point is whether the idea serves the song.

This session moves through the space of production decisions. When should the vocals rest? How should the organ respond? What does the bass need to do to support without overwhelming? These are arrangement questions, not technical questions. The tools — Scaler, sidechain compression, gating, filtering — are just the means of answering them.

The song is simple. The production is deliberate. The result sits somewhere between country sincerity and electronic precision. That is the Folk Sequence space.
