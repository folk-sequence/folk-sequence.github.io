The word folktronica showed up in the late nineteen-nineties in the British music press, applied first to Kieran Hebden, the producer who records as Four Tet. The press needed a label for what Hebden was doing on records like Rounds and Pause, which were full of acoustic instruments cut up and rearranged with the techniques of electronic dance music. Then the same word got applied to Caribou, to The Books, to Tunng, to Beth Orton's stranger records, and for about five years it was the genre tag everyone in indie circles wanted to either claim or argue about.

By the late two-thousands the word had drifted out of fashion. Most of the artists associated with it disowned the label, in the way that artists always disown the labels critics put on them. The word started feeling dated, the kind of thing a writer for an alt-weekly would have used in two thousand and four, and by two thousand and twelve nobody serious wanted to be caught using it. By two thousand and twenty it was a Wikipedia entry that nobody updated.

I think this was a mistake, and I think the mistake matters because the territory the word pointed at is still where most of the interesting work is being done, and the people doing it have no shared vocabulary because they all decided the available vocabulary was embarrassing.

Here is the underlying point. Folktronica was never a phase or a scene or a moment. It was the early version of a much longer conversation about what happens when acoustic instruments and electronic production stop being treated as opposites. The artists who got the label first happened to be the early ones, and the press happened to need a word, and the word happened to be ugly. The conversation kept going. The word fell off. The conversation is still where the work is.

Look at what the conversation has produced in the last decade. The lo-fi beats scene is folktronica with a chillhop coat of paint and a different demographic. The whole ambient folk movement is folktronica with the drum machines turned off. Most of what the music press calls indietronica is folktronica with smaller hats and more reverb. The bedroom producers on Bandcamp who use a sample of an acoustic guitar and a soft drum loop and a field recording of rain are doing folktronica without ever using the word, because the word died before they were old enough to encounter it. The genre did not end. It dispersed into half a dozen other genres, kept all of its actual properties, and lost only the name.

It is worth being precise about what the actual properties were. Folktronica is not just the literal combination of acoustic instruments and electronic production. Plenty of music combines those without being folktronica. A pop song with a programmed beat and an acoustic guitar solo is not folktronica. A folk song with a synthesizer pad in the background is not folktronica. A drum-and-bass track with a sampled fiddle is not folktronica. The combination is necessary but not sufficient.

The actual property is something closer to a sensibility. Folktronica is what you get when the folk side brings in the storytelling tradition, the comfort with empty space, the trust in small gestures, the willingness to let one melodic idea carry an entire song, and the electronic side brings in the texture and the patience for slow change and the freedom from genre constraints, and neither side dominates. The opposite of folktronica is not folk and it is not electronic music. The opposite of folktronica is any music where one of those two sensibilities is using the other as decoration.

Most of what gets marketed as folk-meets-electronic is decoration in one direction or the other. The pop-folk record with the polite synth pad is using the synth pad as window dressing. The dance record with the fiddle sample is using the fiddle as exotica. Folktronica is the stuff where you cannot tell which side is hosting the party, because both sides agreed to throw the party together. That is rarer than it sounds, and it is what made the early Four Tet records feel like they were doing something nobody else was doing.

The thing I am working on this channel sits in that lineage. I use Bitwig instead of analog tape and a sampler. The sessions go modular more often than they go acoustic, because that is where my hands go when I sit down. But the underlying premise, that you can take a folk sensibility and put it inside an electronic environment without either side losing, is the same premise Hebden was working with on Rounds, and the same premise the kids on Bandcamp are still working with whether they have heard of Four Tet or not.

If the word folktronica bothers you because it sounds like something a critic in two thousand and four would have written, fine, use a different word. Call it ambient folk if you want, or lo-fi acoustic, or just music. The word is not the point. The work is the point, and the work has been quietly continuing for twenty-five years while the people doing it pretended they were not all doing the same thing.
