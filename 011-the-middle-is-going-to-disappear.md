When people in the music industry talk about where music production is heading, they usually frame the question as a continuum. On one end of the continuum is the fully manual approach, where a person plays an instrument and records it and mixes it themselves. On the other end is the fully automated approach, where a generation model produces a finished track in response to a text prompt. In between, on this picture, is a long gradient of partially automated production, where the producer uses some manual techniques and some automated tools and the mix between them shifts year over year toward the automated end as the tools get better. The conventional wisdom is that the gradient is what most people will be doing in five years, and that the question is just where on the gradient you want to position yourself.

The conventional wisdom is wrong, and the way it is wrong matters because it is leading a lot of producers to make a strategic error. The error is treating the gradient as stable, when the gradient is not stable. The gradient is collapsing toward its endpoints, and the middle, where most producers currently sit, is going to be unsustainable in a relatively short period of time. The thing that is going to happen is bifurcation. There are going to be two viable positions in five years and the space between them is going to disappear, and producers who are currently in the middle are going to have to choose which side they are moving toward, whether they want to or not.

Here is the structure of the bifurcation. On one end there is going to be fully automated content. This is the music that gets produced at zero marginal cost by generation models for use in playlists, video soundtracks, podcast beds, gym mixes, study music, sleep music, and the kind of background audio that fills the space behind videos on social media. The economics of this kind of music are going to be brutal. The price will fall toward zero because the production cost is zero and the supply is infinite and there is no scarcity to support a price. The royalty pools that currently pay human producers for similar music are going to be diluted out of existence by the volume. The producers who are currently making this kind of music and getting paid for it are going to find that the market for it has disappeared, not because their music got worse but because the market got flooded with audio files that are good enough for the use case at a price they cannot match.

On the other end there is going to be fully hand-made art. This is music that is the opposite of automated content in every dimension that matters. It is made by specific people whose names are known. It is the trace of decisions that were made in real time by humans with intentions. It is sold and consumed in contexts where the specific person matters, not just the audio file. It is performed live or recorded with visible process. It is, often, slow and inefficient and economically inferior to the automated alternative on a dollar-per-minute-of-audio basis. It survives because it is doing something the automated content cannot do, which is connect a specific listener to a specific maker through the artifact of their work.

The middle, the place where most producers currently live, is the place where production is partially automated and the producer is partially anonymous. The middle is where most commercial music has been made for the last twenty years. The middle is the major-label songwriter camp where four people in a room with a generation model and a stack of preset packs build a song to fit a target playlist. The middle is the bedroom producer using a hundred plugins to make a track that sounds like the genre it belongs to. The middle is the production music library that licenses tracks to advertisers and YouTubers. The middle is what most people are competing in right now.

The middle is collapsing because it cannot defend itself against either end. The fully automated end is going to be cheaper. The fully hand-made end is going to be more meaningful. The middle has neither advantage. The producers in the middle are using enough automation to be vulnerable to total automation, and they are not using enough handcraft to offer the connection that hand-made art offers. They are doing the work but the work has no signature, and the no-signature work is exactly the work that the automated systems are best at replacing, because there is nothing specific to that work that the automated systems would have to reproduce in order to substitute for it.

The opposite of the middle position is not a single position. There are two opposites, and that is the whole point of the argument. The middle has two enemies, and they are pulling in opposite directions, and being good at the middle does not help against either of them. The producers who are going to be okay in five years are the ones who pick a side and commit to it. The producers who are going to lose their livelihoods are the ones who try to stay in the middle by making slightly better middle-of-the-road work. The middle is not a place where you can be good enough to survive. The middle is a place that is being squeezed from both ends until it has no width.

There is a similar idea that often gets confused with this one, which is the idea that automation will eventually replace all music production. This idea has been around for as long as automation has been around, and it has always been wrong, and it is wrong now in a more specific way than it has been wrong before. Automation will not replace all music production, because the demand for hand-made music as an artifact of human intention is going to grow, not shrink, in proportion to how much automated content there is in circulation. The more automated content there is, the more valuable the hand-made stuff becomes, because the hand-made stuff is the only place where the connection-to-a-specific-maker thing is happening, and that thing has demand that the automated content cannot satisfy by definition. The hand-made end of the bifurcation is going to grow. It is just going to grow in a smaller, weirder, more specific way than the middle has been growing.

The strategic question for any producer right now is which side to commit to. The answer depends on what you want from a music practice. If you want to make a living producing audio files at scale, the answer is the automated end, and you should be learning how to use the generation tools well, and you should be acquiring whatever skill is left to acquire in that direction before the skill itself becomes automated. If you want to make work that has a chance of mattering to specific people in a specific way, the answer is the hand-made end, and you should be developing the kind of practice where the specific decisions you are making are visible and slow and irreplaceable. The middle is not an answer. The middle is the question that is going away.

This channel is on the hand-made end. Every video is a screen recording of decisions being made in real time, with no edits and no narration and no shortcuts, in a software environment that I have committed to learning deeply over a long period instead of skimming across the surface of. The economics of this approach are not great in the short term and probably not great in the long term either. The reason I am doing it anyway is that the work has to be the work, and the work is the part where decisions are made, and if the decisions go away then the thing that is left is not the kind of music I am interested in making.

The middle is going to disappear. Pick a side.
